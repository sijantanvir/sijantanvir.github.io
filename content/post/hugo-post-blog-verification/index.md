## Why a Workflow Matters

Most writing blocks are process problems, not ability problems.  
When your workflow is unstable, every paragraph feels harder than it should.

The goal is not to write perfectly in one pass.  
The goal is to create a repeatable pipeline that keeps momentum and quality.

## Phase 1: Capture Ideas Quickly

Start with frictionless capture:

- one note per idea
- one sentence for the core claim
- one sentence for why it matters

Do not optimize wording here. Focus on signal, not polish.

### Minimal Idea Template

Use this template for each new idea:

1. claim
2. evidence needed
3. audience
4. risk or uncertainty

This keeps early thinking compact and comparable.

## Phase 2: Build an Outline Before Drafting

Drafting without structure usually creates rewrites later.  
A good outline is your decision map.

Create these sections first:

- problem statement
- key argument
- supporting points
- limitations
- conclusion

### Section-Level Questions

For each section, answer:

- What must the reader understand after this section?
- What evidence is required?
- What is the one sentence summary?

If you cannot answer these, the section is not ready to draft.

## Phase 3: Write in Controlled Passes

Use separate passes for separate goals:

1. **Draft pass:** completeness over quality
2. **Clarity pass:** simplify phrasing and flow
3. **Evidence pass:** verify claims, citations, and numbers
4. **Style pass:** tone, consistency, and concision

Mixing all passes at once slows progress and increases cognitive load.

## Phase 4: Add Reproducibility Early

For technical writing, reproducibility should be integrated, not bolted on.

At minimum:

- keep analysis scripts versioned
- store figures in deterministic output folders
- log major analytic choices
- avoid manual edits to generated results

This prevents late-stage integrity issues.

## Phase 5: Review With a Checklist

Before publishing, run a strict checklist:

- heading hierarchy is clean
- claims have matching evidence
- visuals are labeled and referenced
- terminology is consistent
- abstract and conclusion align

A checklist catches preventable errors faster than intuition.

## Final Notes

A strong writing system is compounding infrastructure.  
Each iteration makes the next document easier to produce.

If your current process feels chaotic, simplify the pipeline first.  
Speed and quality usually improve together once structure is stable.
